Billecart-Salmon: The Family House That Masters Délicatesse

In Mareuil-sur-Aÿ, where the Montagne de Reims slopes toward the Marne Valley, Billecart-Salmon has pursued the same quiet philosophy for over two centuries. While neighboring houses chase power and prestige, this family operation — still owned by the Billecart descendants — builds champagnes of remarkable délicatesse. Their rosé, fermented at unusually low temperatures, achieves a crystalline purity that made it the unofficial champagne of French high society.

Founded 1818 Founder Nicolas François Billecart and Elisabeth Salmon Location Mareuil-sur-Aÿ

The marriage of Nicolas François Billecart and Elisabeth Salmon in 1818 created more than a family — it birthed a champagne philosophy. Combining Billecart's merchant acumen with the Salmon family's vineyard holdings in Mareuil-sur-Aÿ, they established a house dedicated to gentle winemaking. Where others pressed hard for extraction, they pressed softly. Where others fermented warm for speed, they chose cool temperatures for finesse. This patience became the family signature, passed through seven generations who expanded slowly, carefully, always prioritizing quality over growth.

The Philosophy
Délicatesse above all — lightness, finesse, and elegance through gentle handling and family tradition.

Billecart-Salmon operates by one principle: délicatesse through gentle handling. Every process — from pressing to riddling to dégorgement — happens slowly, carefully. They ferment at lower temperatures than most houses, preserving the wine's natural aromatics. The remuage still happens by hand in their crayères, and they age wines longer than required because the family believes great champagne cannot be rushed. This philosophy extends to their vineyard management across 100 hectares, where they practice sustainable viticulture not from trend but from respect for the terroir that has sustained seven generations.

The Billecart-Salmon rosé technique deserves mention — they achieve that pale copper color not through extended skin contact but through cold fermentation and precise assemblage. Most rosé champagne comes from saignée or brief maceration. They blend red wine with white, but ferment everything at 10-12°C, half the temperature of typical champagne production.

Visit

Private cellars dating to 1818, hand riddling demonstrations in historic crayères, and guided tasting of three signature cuvées. Tours emphasize family winemaking traditions and low-temperature fermentation techniques.

40 Rue Carnot, 51160 Mareuil-sur-Aÿ, France
