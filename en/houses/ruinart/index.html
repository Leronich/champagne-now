House Profile

Ruinart: The Oldest Champagne House

When Nicolas Ruinart established his house in 1729, he created not just the first Champagne house, but a template for elegance that endures today. Guided by his uncle Dom Thierry Ruinart's prescient vision of sparkling wine's commercial potential, Ruinart has spent nearly three centuries perfecting the expression of chardonnay in its purest form.

Founded 1729 Founder Nicolas Ruinart Location Reims

The Ruinart story begins not with Nicolas, but with his uncle Dom Thierry Ruinart, a Benedictine monk whose scholarly travels revealed sparkling wine's growing popularity among European aristocracy. In the early 1700s, Dom Thierry shared his nephew his conviction that this effervescent wine represented commerce's future. Nicolas, a successful cloth merchant, waited until royal permission allowed Champagne transport by bottle rather than barrel before founding his house in 1729. This patience proved wise—Ruinart quickly became the preferred Champagne of European courts, its reputation built on the monk's insight and the merchant's commercial acumen.

The Philosophy
The art of chardonnay, preserved through three centuries in the world's deepest crayères.

Ruinart's philosophy centers on chardonnay's ability to express terroir with transparent elegance. Their approach favors finesse over power, seeking wines that whisper rather than shout. The house maintains that chardonnay, when handled with restraint, reveals Champagne's most aristocratic character. This translates to minimal intervention winemaking, extended lees aging in their historic crayères, and blending that emphasizes harmony over complexity. Each wine reflects not just the grape, but the chalky subsoil that has sheltered their wines for three centuries.

Those ancient crayères aren't just atmospheric—they maintain a constant 11°C that's actually optimal for slow, steady aging. Most houses store reserve wines in steel tanks now, but Ruinart's commitment to these galleries shows in their wines' distinctive mineral backbone.

Visit

Descend into UNESCO-listed crayères carved by Romans in the 1st century, where bottles age in galleries extending 38 meters underground. The tour includes tasting in chambers where temperature hasn't varied in 2,000 years.

4 Rue des Crayères, 51100 Reims

Where to Stay

Les Crayères — A château hotel literally built above champagne cellars, with Michelin-starred dining and gardens designed for post-tasting contemplation.
