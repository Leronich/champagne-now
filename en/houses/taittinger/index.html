House Profile

Taittinger: Aristocratic Elegance in Ancient Cellars

Pierre Taittinger acquired his Champagne house in 1932, drawn by cellars that had already witnessed sixteen centuries of winemaking. Today, these Gallo-Roman chalk galleries stretch 18 kilometers beneath Reims, aging wines that favor Chardonnay's mineral precision over power. The family maintains this aristocratic restraint across their range, from their entry Brut Réserve to the legendary Comtes de Champagne.

Founded 1932 Founder Pierre Taittinger Location Reims

Pierre Taittinger's path to Champagne began during the Great War, when he was billeted at the Château de la Marquetterie in Épernay. The cavalry officer fell in love with the region's wines and landscape. After the armistice, he built a successful business empire, then in 1932 seized the opportunity to purchase the Forest-Fourneaux house and its precious cellars. His vision: wines that would embody French elegance rather than mere richness.

The Philosophy
Chardonnay leads the dance here — sometimes accounting for 40% of their blends where others use 30%.

The Taittinger style prioritizes finesse over force. Their Chardonnay-forward approach creates wines with crystalline clarity — you taste the grape, the chalk, the year, in that order. They own 288 hectares across premier and grand cru sites, allowing them to maintain consistency while expressing vintage variation. The long aging in their ancient cellars, some carved by Romans in the 4th century, adds subtle complexity without overwhelming the fruit. Each wine should feel effortless to drink, though the winemaking behind that ease is anything but simple.

I've noticed Taittinger ages differently than most houses. Where others develop toastiness after five years, these wines maintain their mineral edge for decades. The Comtes de Champagne from the 1990s still tastes like liquid chalk — in the best possible way.

Visit

The historic cellars tour descends into Gallo-Roman chalk galleries, some of the oldest underground spaces in Champagne. You'll see 4th-century stone carvings alongside modern riddling gyropalettes, then taste three cuvées including the Comtes de Champagne.

9 Place Saint-Nicaise, 51100 Reims

Where to Stay

Villa Demoiselle — This restored 1900s villa combines Art Nouveau elegance with modern comfort, just minutes from Taittinger's cellars in central Reims.
