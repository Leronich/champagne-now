Demi-Sec: The Sweetest Expression of Champagne

Champagne with 32-50 grams per liter of residual sugar, making it the sweetest style in the hierarchy of dosage.

Before the mid-19th century, all champagne was sweet — demi-sec represents the original taste preference of champagne's first consumers. Russian nobility particularly favored these sweeter styles, and many houses still produce demi-sec specifically for markets that appreciate this fuller, rounder expression.

In the Glass

The first impression is immediate sweetness, but not cloying — it settles on the mid-palate with honeyed weight. The bubbles feel softer, almost creamy, carrying flavors of baked apple, candied citrus peel, and brioche soaked in golden syrup. The finish lingers with gentle warmth, balancing the sugar against champagne's natural acidity.

What to Look For

The sugar should integrate seamlessly with the wine's structure — avoid bottles where sweetness feels added rather than harmonious. Look for producers who age their demi-sec longer, as the sweetness develops complexity with time. The best examples maintain champagne's signature acidity despite the higher dosage.

At the Table
Fresh fruit tarts or crème brûlée

The wine's sweetness complements dessert without competing, while champagne's acidity cuts through rich custard and caramelized sugar

✦

Most champagne houses stopped producing demi-sec regularly after the 1960s when brut became fashionable. The few that continue often use it as a showcase for their dosage skills — it's much harder to hide flaws when there's that much residual sugar.
