The Aube: Champagne's Southern Region

Two hours southeast of Épernay, the Aube unfolds as rolling hills dotted with red-tiled farmhouses. Pinot Noir vines stretch toward the Burgundy border, their leaves catching afternoon light differently here—warmer, longer shadows. Village signs read like a wine map: Les Riceys, Celles-sur-Ource, Bar-sur-Seine. This is Champagne's largest region by area, yet its quietest.

Primary Grape Pinot Noir (85% of plantings) Classification Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée
The Chalk

The Aube sits on Kimmeridgian clay mixed with limestone—the same geological foundation as Chablis. These heavy soils retain water well, perfect for Pinot Noir's needs. The warmer continental climate ripens fruit more reliably than the northern regions, though September rains can challenge harvest timing.

The Aube produces nearly 25% of all Champagne grapes while remaining refreshingly unpretentious. Here, growers still sell directly from cellar doors, prices remain reasonable, and Les Riceys offers France's rarest wine experience—the only village making still red, still rosé, and sparkling wine under three separate appellations. The terroir favors structure over elegance, power over finesse.

Worth Knowing
Champagne Fleury

Biodynamic pioneers since 1989, their Blanc de Noirs shows what Aube Pinot Noir can achieve with conviction

Alexandre Bonnet

Four generations perfecting Les Riceys terroir, their rosé still wine captures summer in clay soils

Champagne Drappier

Based in Urville since 1808, masters of low-sulfur winemaking with remarkable cellar aging potential

✦

The Aube's reputation as Champagne's value region undersells its terroir potential. These clay-limestone soils produce Pinot Noir with genuine aging capacity—something worth remembering when the northern regions' prices climb ever higher.

If You Go

Les Riceys village for its three-wine appellation rarity. The Drappier cellars in Urville showcase traditional methods. Bar-sur-Seine offers excellent grower tastings without appointment pressure. The countryside drives between Celles-sur-Ource and Polisot reveal Champagne's agricultural heart.

Best time: September for harvest energy, or May-June when Pinot Noir flowers perfume the warmer air

Stay Nearby

Hostellerie Les Cèdres, Bar-sur-Seine
